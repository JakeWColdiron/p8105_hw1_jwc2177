### iv. Graphs!

Now that we’re more familiar with the data, let’s elucidate more
information with a scatter plot. In this instance, we’re comparing the
how the time_hour variable affects the tempature variable, and we’re
grouping each data point by the humdity level.

``` r
# Our First Scatter Plot

ggplot(early_january_weather, aes(x = time_hour, y = temp, color = humid)) + geom_point(size = 3)
```

![](p8105_hw1_jwc2177_files/figure-gfm/Our%20First%20Scatter%20Plot-1.png)<!-- -->
The data is an overall strong, positive correlation but there are
fluxes. As one might expect, during the night time the temperature drops
and so does the humidity, and as the day time arrives so the temperature
and humidity increases.

Interestingly on the right part if the graph before 14 January, we must
presume there must have been a significant weather event. While the days
previously repeated, this over this day the temperature and humidity
stabilized regardless of time. Therefore, this could have been a severe
multi-day storm that stabilized the weather. This is further reinforced
by the spike after the stable period and the subsequent drop to normal
temperature.
